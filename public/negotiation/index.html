Question three

How do you keep the price
as low as possible?

Negotiation makes a lot of buyers uneasy, because they picture confrontation — haggling, awkwardness, the risk of looking cheap. Real negotiation is almost the opposite. It's quiet, informed, and unemotional. It's reading the situation accurately and using what you know. The buyers who pay the least are rarely the most aggressive; they're the best informed.

Read the seller before you read the price

Every listing leaks information about how badly the seller wants out:

  • Days on market. A home that's lingered has fewer suitors and a more nervous seller than one listed last week.
  • Price reductions. A drop — especially more than one — tells you the original number was wrong and the seller knows it.
  • Relisting history. A property pulled and re-listed often means a sale fell through, and a twice-bitten seller is a more cautious one.
  • Why they're selling. A probate sale, a relocation, or a broken chain all create motivation that has nothing to do with the house itself.

The more motivated the seller, the more room there is beneath the asking price.

Your own position is leverage too

Sellers don't just want the highest number — they want certainty. Being a clean, proceedable buyer is worth real money: a mortgage agreed in principle, no chain (or a short one), and flexibility on completion timing can all justify a lower price than a higher offer from a riskier buyer. Make your strengths visible.

A few principles that pay for themselves

Lead with a considered offer and justify it with evidence, not emotion — pointing to what comparable homes actually sold for is far harder to dismiss than "it's all we can afford." Don't negotiate against yourself: make your offer, then let it sit. Silence is a tool. And remember who you're talking to — the estate agent is paid by the seller, however friendly they are, so share your enthusiasm carefully. Falling in love out loud is expensive.

This works best when your number is already sound, so set your offer before you negotiate and you'll be defending a position you can stand behind.

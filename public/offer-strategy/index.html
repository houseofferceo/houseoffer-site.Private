Question two

What should you offer
to win?

Knowing what a home is worth is not the same as knowing what to offer. Value is a fact about the market. Your offer is a strategic decision about you — your budget, your appetite for risk, and how much you want this particular house.

Get it wrong in one direction and you overpay: you hand the seller thousands you never needed to. Get it wrong in the other and you lose the home to someone who simply read the situation better. Most buyers only ever feel one of these fears at a time — and whichever one is loudest in the moment quietly drives the decision. A good offer strategy keeps both in view.

The two risks, held in balance

Think of every offer as a trade-off between two costs: the money you risk wasting by going too high, and the home you risk losing by going too low. The smartest offer isn't the lowest possible number — it's the one that minimises your total expected regret. Low enough to protect your money, high enough to realistically win.

Where that point sits depends on a handful of things:

  • How the asking price compares to true value. Start by checking what it's worth — an offer only makes sense relative to the evidence.
  • Demand and competition. A property that's just hit the market in a hot area behaves very differently from one that's been sitting for three months.
  • Seller motivation. A motivated seller and a casual one will accept very different numbers for the same house.
  • Your genuine ceiling. The number above which you'd rather walk away — decided calmly, in advance, not in the heat of a bidding war.

Your first offer sets the table

Your opening number anchors the entire negotiation that follows. Come in too low and you can offend a seller into digging in; come in at full asking and you've signalled there's more in the tank. A considered first offer, backed by data you can point to, does two jobs at once: it protects your position and it tells the agent you're a serious, informed buyer rather than a chancer.

Once your offer is set, the job shifts to protecting it — that's where you negotiate the price down and hold your line through the back-and-forth.

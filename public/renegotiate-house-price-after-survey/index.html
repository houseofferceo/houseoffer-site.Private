How to renegotiate a house price after a survey

The short answer: yes, you can renegotiate after a survey, and a survey that uncovers real problems is one of the strongest grounds you have. The key is to negotiate from evidence — written quotes for the work required — not from alarm. A calm, costed request to reduce the price or share the repair cost is reasonable, expected, and frequently successful.

Can you renegotiate after the survey?

You can. Your offer was accepted on the information available at the time; a survey exists precisely to surface what you couldn't see. If it reveals defects, or the cost of putting them right, that materially changes what the home is worth to you, you're entitled to go back to the seller. Nothing is binding until contracts exchange.

What justifies a reduction

Three things carry weight with a seller:

  • Defects the survey found — damp, structural movement, a roof near the end of its life, dated wiring, Japanese knotweed. The more specific and documented, the stronger.
  • The cost of the work — a defect is a talking point; a written quote is a negotiating position. "The survey mentions damp" invites a shrug. "Here are two quotes averaging £6,400 to treat and make good" invites a number.
  • A mortgage down-valuation — if your lender's valuer says the home is worth less than you offered, the lender will only lend against the lower figure. That's an independent third party agreeing your price was high, and it's some of the most persuasive leverage there is.

How to do it

Get quotes first. One or two written estimates for the significant issues turn your case from opinion into arithmetic. Then put the request in writing to the agent, calmly: reference the survey, attach the costs, and state your revised figure or your ask (full reduction, split the difference, or seller completes the work before exchange). Give the seller a clear, reasonable number to respond to.

Then hold your nerve. You don't have to reply the moment they push back. A short silence is not weakness — it signals you're prepared to walk if the price no longer makes sense.

What not to do

Don't inflate minor findings into a crisis — sellers and agents read surveys too, and an unreasonable demand damages your credibility. Don't renegotiate on things you knew before you offered; that reads as bad faith. And don't threaten to walk unless you actually would — an empty ultimatum called out is a weak place to negotiate from.

Know your real number before you go back

A survey renegotiation is only as strong as the evidence behind it. Before you reopen the price, it's worth knowing what the home is genuinely worth on the current market — comparable sold prices, adjusted to today — so your revised offer is anchored in data, not just the survey shock.
